CSS: The Box Model

Think about the screens you have come across daily. Most of them are probably rectangular. When you open a website in a browser window most of them tend to divide their content in rectangles, however there are some exceptions. In daily life, you might come across a round LCD screen on the face of a watch or maybe a speedometer in your car (they seem to add a ‘futuristic’ feel in vehicles, I mean, check out the inside of the Iron man Helmet).


Like I mentioned earlier, on the web, most content is divided into rectangular sections. Circles within a rectangular monitor are usually style choices over real usability and performance enhancers. Most websites are also made of two types of languages that make up the content and design of the page- HTML and CSS. The skeleton of a webpage is HTML. It is the bones, the structure, that also holds website content. CSS is the skin and makeup. Most screens are rectangular, most browsers are rectangular, hence CSS divisions are made using a Box Model.


The box model representing margin, padding, and border.

What is the box model? Look at the image below. It represents the box model which is an arrangement of rectangular boxes that help divide content on a webpage. Each box has four sides effectively named top, right, bottom, and left, in that order. Think clockwise. The space inside the box is divided up in to a cascading group of boxes that are placed inside each other, these are: margin, border, and padding. These three divisions are vital to placement of objects in our browser window.

The margin is outer most box, it is unique because it doest resize the the box itself, but rather, it determines how other boxes interact with this division of content. Let me show you what I mean. The three boxes below are styled using CSS and they are given a margin of “0 auto”.

Each box in the CSS above (‘#box1’, ‘#box2’, and ‘#box3’) is given a margin of 0 auto, which essentially tells the computer to automatically set all margins to 0, effectively making the margin disappear. This is why there is no space between our boxes. Next to follow in our style pattern is padding. Padding is the space between our border and our content, (in this example our text inside the box is our content). Our box padding is set to 20px; this sets the space between all sides of our content to 20px wide. But our text is left justified, which creates the space on our right. Lastly our border. The border tag takes 3 very different arguments, the first is ‘2px’ this defines our border width. Next our border is solid, you can choose others such as dashed, or dotted, or even custom border images.. Lastly our border color is black.

So there you have it, margin controls how the boxes interact with each other.

At this point you are probably thinking to yourself, if margin, padding, and border don’t change the size of the box, what does? In CSS there are a couple different ways to represent box size, they can also be determined relative to our browser size. The default size of boxes, that have no size declaration is set to fill our entire browser window while accommodating each margin size. One way to set box size is by setting box ‘height’ and ‘width’. There are many different ways to define content size and position on a browser window, spacing is always defined by our box model consisting of padding, border, and margin. The thing to keep in mind is, each box can be styled differently and they all have a certain amount of padding, border, and margin. Go forth and experiment!
